About This Capstone

The aim of this project is to explain the long-term significance of the end of the Negro Leagues on African American participation in baseball. The Negro Leagues were an important example of black enterprise, borne from the deliberate exclusion of African Americans from white leagues.

It was African Americans' answer to the color ban. Those who still wanted to play the game they loved did not allow the unfair ban to deter them. The maxim "constraints breed resourcefulness" is apropos. Early teams first took to barnstorming, traveling around and challenging white teams to exhibition, establishing a large audience. Important events such as World War I and the Great Migration drove the need for baseball as entertainment, which the Negro Leagues readily provided.

After several unsuccessful attempts, the Negro National League was formed in 1920, with Rube Foster, the strict manager of the Chicago American Giants, steering the ship. It proved a successful endeavor and created a system where African Americans participated fully in all facets of baseball: supporting staff, players, coaches, managers, and owners. Yet, the desire for integrated ball persisted.

However, the intentional way MLB elected to integrate, without any consideration or possible partnerships from the Negro Leagues, caused the demise. It created a domino effect accelerating the demise of other Black-owned businesses that had developed as a result of having a baseball team as well as a diminished cultural significance of baseball to the African American culture. The sport that had once been deeply woven into the culture lost its relevance as it was no longer being passed down to younger generations.
