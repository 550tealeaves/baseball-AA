Negro National League (NNL I) (1920-1931)

The first Negro National League was the brainchild of renowned pitcher and team owner, Rube Foster. An astute businessman, Foster noted that during the waning years of WWI, the Great Migration brought affluent African Americans up north. An all-black league would be a great source of entertainment. Indeed the first league was a success, popularizing the likes of Satchel Paige, Buck Leonard, Josh Gibson, Oscar Charleston, and Turkey Stearns. The NNL was one of 7 Negro Leagues that formed in this time period. Overall, the Negro Leagues created an entire business economy, where the owners, coaches, players, and consumers were predominantly black.

  • 1920-1965

    Formation of Kansas City Monarchs

    Created by the only white owner in the Negro Leagues, J.L. Wilkinson, this was longest running franchise in the Negro Leagues. It was one of 8 teams originally in the NNLI. Among their many achievements is developing a method for night games using a lighting system, long before MLB. They won 10 championships, had only 1 season with a losing record (1944), and produced the most major league players in the Negro Leagues15,16
    Notable players included:

    • Jackie Robinson (HOF)
    • Bullet Rogan (HOF)
    • Oscar "Heavy" Johnson
    • Hurley McNair
    • Newt Joseph
    • Bill Foster (HOF)
    • Satchel Paige (HOF)

  • February 13, 1920-1931

    Formation of Negro National League

    This was the first successful attempt at organizing Black baseball teams. Foster served as president, and the league was designed to mimic elements of the MLB. The first game of the new league was held on May 2, 1920, and nearly 8000 spectators watched as the Indianapolis ABCs routed the Chicago Giants 4-2. 17,18
    Original 8 teams were:

    • Chicago American Giants
    • Chicago Giants
    • Cuban Stars
    • Dayton Marcos
    • Detroit Stars
    • Indianapolis ABCs
    • Kansas City Monarchs
    • St. Louis Giants

  • 1920-1936

    Formation of the Negro Southern League (NSL)

    This minor league team, formed by Tom Wilson, served as a feeder team for the major Negro Leagues, serving as a de-facto a major league team in 1932.19
    Original 8 teams were

    • Atlanta Black Crackers
    • Birmingham Black Barons
    • Jacksonville Stars
    • Knoxville Giants
    • Montgomery Grey Sox
    • Nashville White Sox
    • New Orleans Caulfield Ads
    • Pensacola Giants

  • 1923-1928

    Formation of the Eastern Colored League (ECL)

    Created by Nat Stong, one of the few white owners, the ECL originally competed with the NNL I for prominent players. By 1924, the two leagues resolved their issues and created the Colored World Series (2nd pic) that would play until 1927.20
    Original 6 teams were

    • Bacharach Giants
    • Baltimore Black Sox
    • Brooklyn Royal Giants
    • Cuban Stars of the East
    • HIlldale Baseball Club
    • Lincoln Giants of New York

  • 1924

    First Colored World Series aka Negro World Series

    Played in the fall of 1924 between ECL's Hilldale Baseball Club and NNL I's Kansas City Monarchs, this series featured legendary talents, such as Judy Johnson, Biz Mackey, Oscar "Heavy" Johnson, Newt Joseph, Frank Duncan, and Bullet Joe Rogan. A long 10-game series, played at Schorling Park in Chicago, resulted in KC beating Hilldale 5-0. The winning team earned approximately $308 each (~$5,400 today) and the losing team $193 each (~3,400). Fan interest was of utmost importance, and this series drew about 45,000 fans.21

  • 1929

    Formation of the American Negro League (ANL)

    This league operated in the East Coast and was occupied the void created when the ECL folded. It had a split season, with two half schedules and post-season. While shortlived, the ANL tried to accurately compile player statistics.22
    Original 6 teams were

    • Bacharach Giants
    • Baltimore Black Sox
    • Cuban Stars
    • HIlldale Baseball Club
    • Homestead Grays
    • Lincoln Giants

  • December 9, 1930

    NNL founder, Rube Foster, died

    Following a lengthy illness, Foster passed away at age 51. His death accelerated the inevitable decline of the NNL. Joe Green said of passing, "when Rube Foster died, Negro baseball died with him."23

  • 1931

    End of the NNL I

    Primarily the result of a void in leadership after Foster's death and the impact of the Great Depression.17,18

  • 1932

    NSL became the last league standing

    Originally a minor league, the NSL was promoted to Negro major league in 1932 and was the new site for the teams that had left the NNL I.19
