Integration

On the day Jackie Robinson broke the color barrier, it had been approximately 63 years since the last black man, Moses Fleetwood Walker, had played for the majors. Brooklyn Dodgers general manager is credited for bringing in Robinson, but the process of integration was ploddingly slow and carefully planned.

  • 1939-1945

    World War II led to the loss of good baseball players

    By the time many of those players returned, they were no longer in their physical prime. Branch Rickey realized the necessity of planning baseball's future by looking towards the Negro Leagues. He framed integration as inevitable and profitable.
    “From Buckeye to Black Eye: Cleveland’s 1945 Negro League Championship, Jackie Robinson, and the Beginning of the End of Black Baseball.”

  • November 25, 1944

    MLB Commissioner, Kenesaw Mountain Landis, died

    Landis, baseball's first commissioner, had long opposed any notions of integrating the league. His successor, Happy Chandler, was more open to the idea.

  • March 12, 1945

    NY passes the Ives-Quinn Act and the Fair Employment Practices Act

    Banned discrimination in employment. This gave Branch Rickey the legal permission to recruit black players.

  • 1945

    Cleveland Buckeyes swept the Homestead Grays in the Negro League World Series

    There was a notable lack of celebration for this accomplishment. Times were changing, and the Negro Leagues would soon be deemed a relic of Jim Crow. “From Buckeye to Black Eye: Cleveland’s 1945 Negro League Championship, Jackie Robinson, and the Beginning of the End of Black Baseball.”

  • 1933-1948

    Formation of the Negro National League II (NNL II)

    Established by Greenlee, this league was a bit more chaotic than its predecessor, featuring an erratic schedule that saw teams play an unequal amount of games each season. In the 1940s, they held the Negro World Series. Despite the talented players, financial mismanagement, disputed records, and primarily the integration in the MLB would lead to its collapse in 1948.
    Original 7 teams were

    • Baltimore Black Sox
    • Chicago American Giants
    • Columbus Blue Birds
    • Indianapolis ABCs
    • Homestead Grays
    • Nashville Elite Giants
    • Pittsburgh Crawfords

    Source
  • September 10, 1933

    1st East-West All-Star game at Comiskey Park

    Gus Greenlee came up with this game, which featured the Negro National League II (East) vs Negro American League (West). At least 10 players and a manager would later be inducted into MLB's Hall of Fame. Featured the likes of Turkey Stearnes, Hilton Smith, Jim West etc. The West routed the East 11-7. Source

  • 1942-1948

    Negro World Series

    Originally played during the first NNL, from 1924-1927, this popular post-season game returned during the second iteration. Perennial post-season teams included the Homestead Grays and Birmingham Black Barons. Source

  • November 25, 1944

    MLB Commissioner, Kenesaw Mountain Landis, died

    Landis had long opposed any notions of integrating the league. His successor, Happy Chandler, was more open to the idea.

  • 1945

    Jackie Robinson played for Kansas City Monarchs

    He played one year in the Negro Leagues before being acquired by the Montreal Royals.

  • March 1946 - July 1946

    Formation of West Coast Negro Baseball Association

    Founder of the Harlem Globetrotters, Abe Saperstein, was the president.
    This short term league had 6 teams:

    • Los Angeles White Sox
    • Oakland Larks
    • Portland Rosebuds
    • San Diego Tigers
    • San Francisco Sea Lions
    • Seattle Steelheads
