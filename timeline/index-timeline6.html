End of the Negro Leagues (1948-1960s)

Once the color barrier broke and owners realized the profits they could make acquiring black players, the fate of the Negro Leagues was sealed. Long plagued with economic hardships, stemming from the effects of Jim Crow, the Negro Leagues pushed forward in spite of it all. Beginning its economic collapse, the MLB plundered the Negro Leagues of their best players, causing a mass exodus of black fans who opted to follow their favorite players to the white leagues. The loss of both assets (players) and revenue (customers) drove many of the leagues to a quick bankruptcy or forced owners to sell at a heavy losses. Moreover, the message changed and the Negro Leagues were villainized as a symbol of America's racist past that they so desperately wanted to "move on" from. The painful irony is that the Negro Leagues, once a symbol of the sheer ingenuity of black enterprise, could no longer exist because it reminded the MLB its cruel actions.

  • 1948

    End of the Negro National League II

    The end of the NNL II marks the end of an era of major black enterprise, never to return to baseball. It had 6 teams (Homestead Grays, Baltimore Elite Giants, Newark Eagles, Philadelphia Stars, New York Cubans, & New York Black Yankees), half of which went bankrupt. Source.

  • 1949

    Some of the teams from the defunct NNLII joined the Negro American League (NAL)

    The Baltimore Elite Giants, Philadelphia Stars, and the NY Cubans joined the Negro American League. Source2

  • 1950s

    Domino effect of teams collapsing

    As the best players moved to the MLB, the fans migrated over, as well. Negro League team owners struggled to sell tickets. The quality of play declined, and several teams either went bankrupt or were sold at a huge loss.

  • 1945

    Formation of the United States League (USL)

    Created by Branch Rickey, this league served as a cover for him to scout the black player that would eventually break the color line. Jackie Robinson never played here.

  • October 23, 1945

    Branch Rickey signed Jackie Robinson to the Montreal Royals

    Robinson was carefully scouted to be the man to break the color line. He had to remain poised and dispassionate in the face of extreme racial animus. Rickey portrayed the Negro Leagues as a racketeer operation instead of a legitimate business to justify his refusal to pay the KC Monarchs for Robinson. This set the precedent of MLB teams stripping the Negro Leagues of their best players without proper compensation.

    “From Buckeye to Black Eye: Cleveland’s 1945 Negro League Championship, Jackie Robinson, and the Beginning of the End of Black Baseball.”

  • April 15, 1947

    Jackie Robinson debuted for the Brooklyn Dodgers, breaking the color line after six decades

    Approximately 26,000 fans bore witness to this event, about 14,000 of whom were Black. During his rookie year, he battled an incredible amount of racism from fans, opposing players, and even his own teammates. However, he proved to be worth the gamble that Rickey made, maintaining his dignity in the face of such prejudice. Source

  • July 5, 1947

    Cleveland Indians purchased the rights to Larry Doby from the Newark Eagles, making him the second Black player to integrate

    Unlike Rickey, Cleveland owner, Bill Veeck, at least compensated the Eagles' owners, the Manleys, for Doby, albeit at far below his actual value. Decline and Fall: The Negro Leagues After Integration
